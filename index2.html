    • Introduction

    Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam ( 15 October 1931 - 27 July 2015) was an Indian aerospace scientist and statesman who served as the 11th president of India from 2002 to 2007. He was born and raised in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu and studied physics and aerospace engineering. He spent the next four decades as a scientist and science administrator, mainly at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and was intimately involved in India's civilian space programme and military missile development efforts. He thus came to be known as the Missile Man of India for his work on the development of ballistic missile and launch vehicle technology. He also played a pivotal organisational, technical, and political role in India's Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998, the first since the original nuclear test by India in 1974. Widely referred to as the "People's President", he returned to his civilian life of education, writing and public service after a single term. He was a recipient of several prestigious awards, including the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian honour. While delivering a lecture at the Indian Institute of Management Shillong, Kalam collapsed and died from an apparent cardiac arrest on 27 July 2015, aged 83. Thousands, including national-level dignitaries, attended the funeral ceremony held in his hometown of Rameswaram, where he was buried with full state honours.

    Early Life

    Dr. Abdul Kalam was born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, India. He came from a humble background and faced several challenges in his early life. Despite these challenges, he excelled in academics and pursued a career in aeronautical engineering.

    His dedication and passion for science led him to contribute significantly to India's space research and missile development programs.

    His contributions to science and technology have left a lasting impact on India's development.

    His visionary leadership and relentless pursuit of excellence continue to inspire generations.

    Achievements

    Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam's contributions and achievements include:

    • Playing a pivotal role in the development of India's first satellite launch vehicle, SLV-III.
    • Leading the successful Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998.
    • Guiding India's space research organization, ISRO, as its Director from 1992 to 1997.
    • Receiving numerous awards and honors, including the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian award, in 1997.

    Legacy

    Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam's legacy extends beyond his scientific achievements. He was known for his humility, simplicity, and his ability to connect with people from all walks of life.

    He was a renowned motivational speaker and inspired millions of young minds to dream big and work hard to achieve their goals. Dr. Kalam emphasized the importance of education and encouraged students to pursue careers in science and technology.

    Even after his presidency, Dr. Kalam continued to engage with students and youth, sharing his wisdom and vision for a prosperous and developed India.

    Dr. Abdul Kalam's legacy serves as a guiding light for future generations and continues to inspire individuals worldwide.
